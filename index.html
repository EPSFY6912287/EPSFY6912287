Abstract

The progression of road vehicle powertrains has historically centred on internal combustion engine systems, with modern engineering increasingly shifting toward electrification. This paper evaluates petrol, diesel and electric powertrains with emphasis on thermodynamic efficiency, system complexity, energy management and emissions engineering. Results show that diesel engines provide superior thermal efficiency among combustion systems, while electric drivetrains deliver unmatched energy efficiency and mechanical simplification. However, battery technology and electrical infrastructure remain engineering limitations to widespread adoption.

Introduction

Selection of a vehicle powertrain strongly influences dynamic performance, operating efficiency and environmental impact. Petrol engines operate using the Otto thermodynamic cycle, while diesel engines rely on compression ignition governed by the Diesel cycle (Heywood, 2018). Electric vehicles convert stored electrochemical energy directly into mechanical output through electromagnetic principles, eliminating many thermodynamic losses (Larminie and Lowry, 2012).

Environmental regulation and climate targets have accelerated the development of electrified vehicles. Engineers must assess complete life-cycle performance rather than isolated operating efficiency in order to select appropriate propulsion systems (International Energy Agency, 2023).

Analysis

4.1 Thermodynamic Efficiency

Petrol engines typically achieve brake thermal efficiencies of 25–30% due to throttling losses and limited compression ratios (Heywood, 2018). Diesel engines operate at higher compression pressures, commonly exceeding 20:1, enabling efficiencies approaching 40% (Stone, 2012). Electric motors do not depend on heat conversion and routinely exceed 85% conversion efficiency under steady operating conditions (Chan and Chau, 2001). However, overall well-to-wheel efficiency is reduced by electricity generation and transmission losses (National Grid ESO, 2022).

4.2 Mechanical Complexity & Reliability

Diesel engines require reinforced components to tolerate high-pressure combustion, increasing mass and material usage (Heywood, 2018). Electric drivetrains eliminate multi-speed transmissions and piston assemblies, improving mechanical reliability but increasing reliance on control electronics and thermal management systems (Ehsani et al., 2004).

4.3 Torque & Performance

Diesel engines provide strong low-speed torque for heavy load applications. Petrol engines achieve peak power at high rotational speeds and depend on gearbox ratios for efficiency. Electric motors generate maximum torque at zero speed, improving acceleration and enabling regenerative braking systems that recover kinetic energy during deceleration (Reitz et al., 2020).

4.4 Energy Storage

Liquid fuels possess superior volumetric and gravimetric energy density and permit quick refuelling. Battery- based vehicles require extended charging times and experience gradual capacity degradation (Verma, Kumar and Jain, 2021; U.S. Department of Energy, 2022).

4.5 Emissions

Combustion engines emit carbon dioxide, nitrogen oxides and unburnt hydrocarbons, requiring complex exhaust after-treatment. Electric vehicles generate no direct emissions; however battery production and electricity generation produce indirect environmental impact (Volkswagen AG, 2021; International Energy Agency, 2023).

4.6 Manufacturing

Diesel engines require forged steel components, while EV batteries depend on lithium, cobalt and rare earth metals that introduce supply-chain risk (Verma et al., 2021).

4.7 Infrastructure

Rapid electric vehicle adoption is constrained by charging infrastructure and power grid capacity. Smart charging schemes are required to prevent peak-load overload (National Grid ESO, 2022).

Conclusion

Diesel engines retain excellent efficiency for long-range usage, petrol engines remain cost-effective for consumers, and electric drivetrains offer unmatched energy efficiency and emissions reduction potential. Hybrid vehicles represent a transitional solution as infrastructure and battery systems improve.
