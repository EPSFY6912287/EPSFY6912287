Abstract

The development of road vehicle powertrains has historically centred on internal combustion engine (ICE) technology, with recent innovation shifting toward electrification. This study examines petrol, diesel, and electric propulsion systems from an engineering perspective, evaluating performance, efficiency, emissions, manufacturing complexity, and infrastructure requirements. The engineering trade-offs associated with thermodynamic efficiency, mechanical design, and energy conversion are discussed. Results indicate that while diesel engines offer superior efficiency among combustion systems, electric drivetrains provide substantially higher energy conversion efficiency and simplified mechanical architecture. However, limitations relating to battery energy density and power generation infrastructure remain significant obstacles to full electrification.

Introduction

Powertrain selection is a critical aspect of automotive engineering, influencing vehicle performance, environmental impact, manufacturing cost, and operational efficiency. Traditional petrol and diesel engines are based on mechanical energy conversion through combustion, governed by thermodynamic cycles such as the Otto and Diesel cycles (Heywood, 2018). Electric drivetrains, by contrast, rely on electrochemical energy storage and electromagnetic energy conversion to deliver motion (Larminie and Lowry, 2012).

Ongoing environmental concerns and policy changes have increased pressure on manufacturers to improve fuel economy and reduce emissions (International Energy Agency, 2023). Engineers must now balance power output, vehicle mass, emissions compliance, and reliability when selecting propulsion systems. This article compares petrol, diesel, and electric engines using engineering principles including efficiency, torque behaviour, material demands, and energy losses.

Analysis

Thermodynamic Efficiency

Petrol engines operate under the Otto cycle, where efficiency is limited by compression ratio, heat transfer losses, and incomplete combustion (Heywood, 2018). Diesel engines operate under the Diesel cycle, achieving higher efficiency through increased compression ratios and lean combustion operation (Reitz et al., 2020). Petrol engines typically operate at thermal efficiencies of 25–30%, while diesel engines may reach values approaching 40% under ideal operating conditions (Stone, 2012). By comparison, electric motors achieve efficiencies greater than 85% due to the absence of thermal losses associated with combustion (Chan and Chau, 2001). However, overall system efficiency is dependent on electricity generation and transmission losses, particularly where non-renewable energy sources dominate (National Grid ESO, 2022).

Mechanical Complexity & Reliability

Internal combustion engines require complex mechanical assemblies involving pistons, crankshafts, camshafts, fuel injectors and exhaust systems. Diesel engines demand stronger engine blocks and higher precision components due to elevated cylinder pressures, increasing both cost and mass (Heywood, 2018). Electric drivetrains use significantly fewer moving parts, consisting mainly of battery packs, power electronics and electric motors (Larminie and Lowry, 2012). While this simplicity improves mechanical reliability, dependency on electronic systems introduces vulnerabilities related to inverter failure and battery management system errors (Ehsani et al., 2004).

Torque & Performance

Petrol engines deliver peak power at high engine speeds, requiring multi-speed transmissions to maintain efficiency (Stone, 2012). Diesel engines generate higher torque at lower rotational speeds due to increased combustion pressure, making them suitable for heavy loads and long-distance travel (Reitz et al., 2020) Electric motors deliver maximum torque from zero speed, providing rapid acceleration and greater control accuracy (Chan and Chau, 2001). Regenerative braking systems recover kinetic energy, improving overall efficiency, but also increase the complexity of thermal management within the battery system (Ehsani et al., 2004).

Energy Storage

Petrol and diesel fuels offer extremely high energy density, allowing long driving ranges and rapid refuelling (Stone, 2012). Electric vehicles, in contrast, rely on lithium-ion batteries with much lower specific energy (Verma, Kumar and Jain, 2021). High-rate charging increases internal battery temperatures and accelerates degradation, reducing service life and increasing ownership cost (U.S. Department of Energy, 2022). Battery replacement remains one of the greatest economic and technical constraints limiting widespread EV adoption.

Emissions

Petrol engines emit carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, and hydrocarbons, requiring catalytic converters for emissions control. Diesel engines must use diesel particulate filters and selective catalytic reduction systems to meet regulatory requirements (Reitz et al., 2020). Electric vehicles produce no tailpipe emissions, significantly improving urban air quality. However, lifecycle analyses show that battery manufacturing and power generation still produce environmental burdens (Volkswagen AG, 2021). Electric vehicles provide the greatest emissions benefit only when powered by renewable electricity (International Energy Agency, 2023).

Manufacturing

Internal combustion engines require precision machining and complex production lines. Diesel engines use stronger materials and increased tolerances, raising manufacturing cost (Heywood, 2018). Electric powertrains shift manufacturing complexity to battery production, involving lithium, cobalt and nickel extraction. These processes raise ethical and environmental concerns and may constrain future supply chains (Verma, Kumar and Jain, 2021).

Infrastructure

Global fuel distribution networks allow near-instant refuelling for petrol and diesel vehicles. Electric vehicles require extensive charging infrastructure and grid reinforcement (National Grid ESO, 2022). Large-scale EV adoption will require smart grid integration and demand balancing technologies.

Conclusion

Diesel engines provide the highest thermal efficiency among combustion engines, while petrol engines remain simpler and cheaper to manufacture. Electric powertrains offer superior energy efficiency and reduced mechanical complexity but depend upon advances in battery engineering and electricity generation. Hybrid solutions represent the most viable short-term transition technology (Ehsani et al., 2004). The long-term solution for road transport lies in electrification supported by renewable energy and sustainable materials engineering.
