Abstract

The progression of road vehicle powertrains has historically centred on internal combustion engine (ICE) systems, with modern engineering increasingly shifting toward electrification. This paper evaluates petrol, diesel and electric powertrains with emphasis on thermodynamic efficiency, system complexity, energy management and emissions engineering. Results show that diesel engines provide superior thermal efficiency among ICE systems, while electric drivetrains deliver unmatched energy efficiency and mechanical simplification. However, battery technology and electrical infrastructure remain engineering limitations to widespread EV adoption (Chan and Chau, 2001; Ehsani et al., 2004).

Introduction

Selection of a vehicle powertrain influences performance, environmental impact and life-cycle cost. Petrol engines operate under the Otto cycle, whereas diesel engines follow compression ignition governed by the Diesel cycle (Heywood, 2018). Electric vehicles convert stored electrical energy directly into mechanical output through electromagnetic processes (Larminie and Lowry, 2012). This paper presents an engineering comparison of these systems across efficiency, reliability and infrastructure constraints.

Increasing regulatory pressure and climate policy has accelerated electrification efforts. Engineers must consider full-system efficiency rather than tailpipe emissions alone (International Energy Agency, 2023).

Analysis

4.1 Thermodynamic Efficiency

Petrol engines exhibit brake thermal efficiencies between 25–30% due to throttling losses and low compression ratios (Heywood, 2018). Diesel engines operate at higher compression ratios exceeding 20:1, allowing efficiencies approaching 40% (Stone, 2012). Electric motors convert energy electromagnetically, enabling efficiencies above 85% under nominal conditions (Chan and Chau, 2001). However, grid generation efficiency reduces total well-to-wheel effectiveness (National Grid ESO, 2022).

4.2 Mechanical Complexity and Reliability

Diesel engines require reinforced components for high cylinder pressures, increasing mass and mechanical stress (Heywood, 2018). Electric drivetrains possess significantly fewer moving components, improving system reliability but increasing dependency on power electronics and software integrity (Ehsani et al., 2004).

4.3 Torque Characteristics and Performance

Diesel powertrains generate high low-speed torque making them suitable for heavy-duty usage. Petrol engines operate effectively at higher engine speeds requiring multi-gear transmissions. Electric motors provide instantaneous torque from zero speed and permit energy recovery through regenerative braking, increasing urban efficiency (Reitz et al., 2020).

4.4 Energy Storage and Refuelling

Liquid fuels provide superior energy density and rapid refuelling (Stone, 2012). Battery systems suffer from capacity fade, long charging times and decreased low-temperature performance (Verma, Kumar and Jain, 2021; U.S. Department of Energy, 2022).

4.5 Emissions Engineering

Combustion engines release CO₂ and particulates, requiring exhaust aftertreatment. Electric vehicles eliminate tailpipe output, although battery manufacturing produces substantial greenhouse emissions (Volkswagen AG, 2021; International Energy Agency, 2023).

4.6 Manufacturing and Materials

High-strength steel alloys dominate diesel manufacturing. EVs rely on rare earth metals and lithium-based systems that raise supply chain concerns (Verma et al., 2021).

4.7 Infrastructure Constraints

Electrical grid capacity remains a limiting factor for nationwide EV adoption. Smart charging and off-peak management strategies are required (National Grid ESO, 2022).

Conclusion

Diesel engines remain the most efficient combustion option, petrol engines offer cost-effective accessibility, and electric drivetrains present unmatched system efficiency. Hybrid configurations serve as transitional technologies until infrastructure and battery design mature.
