Abstract

The development of road vehicle powertrains has historically centred on internal combustion engine (ICE) technology, with recent innovation shifting toward electrification. This study examines petrol, diesel and electric propulsion systems from an engineering perspective, evaluating performance, efficiency, emissions, manufacturing complexity and infrastructure requirements. The engineering trade-offs associated with thermodynamic efficiency, mechanical design and energy conversion are discussed. Results indicate that while diesel engines offer superior efficiency among combustion systems, electric drivetrains provide substantially higher energy conversion efficiency and simplified mechanical architecture. However, limitations relating to battery energy density and power generation infrastructure remain significant obstacles to full electrification (Chan and Chau, 2001; Ehsani et al., 2004).

Introduction

Powertrain selection is a critical engineering decision influencing vehicle performance, environmental impact and economic viability. Petrol and diesel engines rely on thermodynamic energy conversion governed by the Otto and Diesel cycles (Heywood, 2018). Electric vehicles instead use electrochemical energy storage and electromagnetic power conversion (Larminie and Lowry, 2012).

Increased environmental concerns and tightening legislation have driven innovation toward low-emission vehicle technologies (International Energy Agency, 2023). Engineers must consider efficiency, durability, materials, infrastructure, and operational performance in modern propulsion system selection.

Thermodynamic Efficiency

Petrol engines are limited by lower compression ratios and heat losses (Heywood, 2018), typically achieving efficiencies of 25–30%, while diesel engines may reach up to 40% (Stone, 2012). Electric motors exceed 85% efficiency owing to reduced mechanical and thermal losses (Chan and Chau, 2001). However, electricity generation losses can reduce overall system efficiency (National Grid ESO, 2022).

Mechanical Complexity and Reliability

Diesel engines require heavy-duty construction for high-pressure operation, increasing mass and cost (Heywood, 2018). Electric powertrains feature fewer moving components, improving mechanical reliability but increasing reliance on electronic control systems (Ehsani et al., 2004).

Torque and Performance

Diesel engines generate high low-speed torque (Reitz et al., 2020). Electric motors achieve peak torque at zero speed (Chan and Chau, 2001). Regenerative braking improves efficiency but increases battery thermal load (Ehsani et al., 2004).

Energy Storage and Refuelling

Liquid fuels provide high energy density and fast refuelling (Stone, 2012). EVs use lithium-ion batteries with significantly lower energy density and degradation concerns (Verma, Kumar and Jain, 2021; U.S. Department of Energy, 2022).

Emissions Engineering

Petrol and diesel engines require exhaust treatment systems (Reitz et al., 2020). EVs produce no tailpipe emissions but still generate lifecycle emissions through battery manufacturing and electricity production (Volkswagen AG, 2021; International Energy Agency, 2023).

Manufacturing and Materials

Diesel engines require high-strength alloys (Heywood, 2018). EV batteries rely on lithium, cobalt and nickel supply chains (Verma et al., 2021).

Infrastructure

Electric vehicle adoption requires charging networks and grid reinforcement (National Grid ESO, 2022).

Conclusion

Diesel engines remain the most efficient combustion systems, petrol engines remain low cost, and EVs offer the greatest sustainability potential. Hybrid systems offer transitional capability until battery technology and grid infrastructure mature (Ehsani et al., 2004).
